## What the ruling does

The Supreme Court did the most consequential thing it can do without saying anything at all: it let a law take effect. In two one-sentence orders, Justice Samuel Alito turned away emergency pleas from the tech industry and a Texas youth group, leaving the State free to enforce SB 2420 — the App Store Accountability Act — while the courts sort out whether it violates the First Amendment. Starting now, Apple and Google must check the age of every Texan who opens an app store, sort minors into age brackets, and get a parent's sign-off before anyone under 18 downloads an app or buys anything inside one.

## An order without reasons

Notice what is missing: a majority opinion, a vote count, a single sentence of reasoning. This is the emergency docket at full throttle. The Court did not tell us why the challengers are likely to lose, only that they get no relief today. That silence is itself a signal — after the Fifth Circuit found Texas "likely to succeed," the Justices saw no emergency worth their intervention. But an unexplained order is a thin thing to build a First Amendment precedent on, and everyone knows it.

## What this favors

This favors the State, and the broader project of treating the app store as a chokepoint for regulating what minors can reach online. Ken Paxton gets to run his experiment; other red states get a template. It disfavors the platforms, who now bear the cost and legal exposure of age-gating an entire marketplace, and it disfavors the free-speech argument that verifying every adult's age to protect children sweeps in far too much protected expression. For now, the child-safety framing beat the First Amendment framing.

## The aftermath

Age verification is live in Texas, and the compliance machinery — ID checks, parental accounts, app-rating tiers — is now real for two of the largest companies on earth. The merits fight moves back to the Fifth Circuit, and given how the stay was granted, Texas enters that round as the favorite. Expect copycat statutes, expect the platforms to argue that a stay is not a merits ruling, and expect this to reach the Court again on a fuller record — where the Justices will eventually have to explain themselves.

## My take

I am uneasy, and not because Texas is obviously wrong about kids. The problem is the method. A law that a district judge found unconstitutional in nearly all its applications is now enforced against millions of people on the strength of an order that explains nothing. Maybe SB 2420 survives; child-safety laws often should. But the First Amendment cost of age-gating the entire internet deserves an argument, not a shrug. The Court had a choice between a hard question and a quiet order. It chose quiet.
