## What the ruling does

The Court got this right, and it got it right for the right reason. By a 6-3 vote, the Justices held that when police forced Google to comb its Location History database for every device that crossed a drawn-on-a-map zone, they conducted a Fourth Amendment search. That is the only honest reading of *Carpenter v. United States*. If seven days of cell-site records reveal "the privacies of life," then a dragnet that fingerprints everyone near a bank robbery — churchgoers, commuters, the merely unlucky — cannot be a constitutional free lunch just because Google holds the data.

## What this favors

This decision favors the citizen over the database. Its quiet revolution is the burial, in the digital context, of the third-party doctrine's most cynical move: the idea that you surrender all privacy the instant a corporation logs your movements. Kagan refused to let the government launder a general search through a private intermediary. Justice Gorsuch, concurring on property-and-positive-law grounds, would go further still — and his vote is a signal that the originalist wing and the pragmatist wing can converge when the surveillance is gaudy enough. The losers are reverse-location dragnets, suspicionless "tower dumps," and the prosecutorial habit of treating Silicon Valley's server farms as an evidence locker.

## The aftermath

Do not mistake a win for a finish line. The Court vacated and remanded; it did not suppress anything. Chatrie still must show the search was *unreasonable*, and the government will press good-faith and particularity arguments on the way back to the Fourth Circuit. Expect a wave of motions to suppress geofence evidence nationwide, a scramble by magistrate judges to demand narrower "geofence" affidavits, and likely a sequel case on what particularity even means when the target is unknown by definition. Alito's dissent — warning that the majority shoehorned a novel investigative tool into an ill-fitting warrant frame, with Barrett adding that the analogy was simply wrong — will become the template for every prosecutor seeking to cabin today's holding.

## My take

The dissenters are not wrong that geofences are awkward to police with a Framing-era vocabulary. But that awkwardness is an argument for judicial caution about *methods*, not for declaring the most sweeping locational dragnet yet invented to be no search at all. A Constitution that protects your ankle-monitor data but not the map of where your phone slept last Tuesday would be a Constitution made of sand. The Court chose the citizen. It should have gone one inch further and resolved reasonableness here — leaving that open invites years of litigation theater — but as a matter of first principles, this is the privacy ruling of the term. Verdict: correct, courageous, and overdue.
