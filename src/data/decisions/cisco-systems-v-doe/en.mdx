## What the ruling does

*Cisco Systems v. Doe* is the Roberts Court doing what it does best with the Alien Tort Statute: narrowing a single 1789 sentence until almost nothing can pass through it. Justice Barrett's 6-3 majority holds two things, and each is lethal on its own. First, federal courts may no longer recognize any **new** cause of action under the ATS — the cautious window *Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain* cracked open in 2004 is now, for practical purposes, nailed shut. Second, neither the ATS nor the Torture Victim Protection Act reaches those who merely aid and abet. For the Doe plaintiffs — who allege Cisco engineered the surveillance architecture used to hunt, jail, and torture Chinese dissidents — that pairing ends the case.

## The doctrine, and why it is too clever

The majority dresses this up as humility: courts, it says, should not invent international-law torts that Congress never wrote. Fine in the abstract. But *Sosa* already imposed the discipline the Court now claims is missing, limiting ATS claims to a narrow set of universally accepted norms. Torture and arbitrary detention are the paradigm cases. By converting *Sosa*'s caution into a flat prohibition, Barrett does not refine the doctrine — she repeals it sub silentio. And the aiding-and-abetting holding is the quieter scandal: it means the company that knowingly builds the machinery of repression is categorically safer than the foot soldier who flips the switch.

## What this favors

This is a charter of immunity for multinational enablers. It favors the firm that sells the database, the filtering system, the targeting tool — so long as someone else does the actual jailing. It privileges corporate distance over moral proximity, and it tells general counsel everywhere that complicity, however deliberate, is now a litigation-free zone in American courts. Jackson, joined by Kagan, sees it plainly: the opinion launders culpability through the word "aiding."

## The aftermath

Expect a wave of dismissals. Every pending ATS suit against a corporate defendant now has a one-paragraph death warrant. Plaintiffs will migrate to state tort law, foreign forums, and the occasional sanctions regime — thinner remedies, all. The pressure shifts to Congress, which could amend the TVPA to reach abettors with a single clause. It will not. The political economy of "American jobs abroad" guarantees inertia.

## My take

The majority's textualism is not frivolous; the statute is genuinely terse, and judicial lawmaking deserves suspicion. But suspicion is not abdication. The Court had a workable middle path in *Sosa* and chose instead to slam the door on the very victims the First Congress meant to reassure. *Doe* is tidy reasoning and an ugly result. The dissent has the better of it, and history will say so.
