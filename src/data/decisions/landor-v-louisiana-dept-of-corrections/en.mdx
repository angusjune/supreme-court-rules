## What the ruling does

Damon Landor did everything the law asks of a person who knows his rights. A Rastafarian who had not cut his hair in nearly two decades, he arrived at a Louisiana intake facility holding a copy of a Fifth Circuit opinion that declared the very practice he feared — shaving a Rastafarian's head — unlawful under RLUIPA. A guard threw the paper in the trash, handcuffed him to a chair, and shaved him bald anyway. Today a 6-3 Court told him he has no remedy in damages against the men who did it. Justice Gorsuch's reasoning is clean, contractual, and, in my judgment, wrong about what justice requires.

## What this favors

This decision favors the abstraction of the Spending Clause over the person it was meant to protect. The logic runs like this: RLUIPA's commands are conditions Congress attached to federal prison money, the State is the only party to that "contract," and a contract cannot bind officials who never signed it. Therefore the guards — the actual hands that did the shaving — owe nothing personally. It is a tidy syllogism that happens to immunize the exact people most able to violate a prisoner's faith and least deterred by a distant threat to the warden's budget. It rewards the institution and pardons the individual.

## The aftermath

The practical fallout is a remedial black hole. Injunctions are useless to an inmate who has already been shorn or transferred; sovereign immunity, courtesy of *Sossamon*, blocks damages against the State itself. Strip out individual-capacity suits and RLUIPA becomes a statute with a right but no teeth — a promise enforceable only while the harm is ongoing and the plaintiff still inside. Contrast *Tanzin v. Tanvir*, where a unanimous Court read RFRA to allow exactly these damages against federal officers. The majority waves off the parallel by pointing to RLUIPA's Spending Clause pedigree, but Landor will not appreciate that his faith was worth less because Congress used a different enumerated power.

## My take

Doctrinally, Gorsuch has a real argument; morally, the Court flinched. When a guard knowingly defies clearly established law and the victim is left to mouth the words "no damages," something has gone wrong with our theory of remedies. The fix now sits with Congress, which can amend RLUIPA to spell out individual liability the way the majority demands — and it should, quickly. Until then, the message to every official tempted to trample an inmate's religion is unmistakable: do it, and the worst that happens is the State loses a check it will never actually lose. Verdict: legally defensible, but a defeat for the rule of law it claims to honor.
