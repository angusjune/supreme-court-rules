## What the ruling does

Strip away the doctrinal varnish and *Monsanto v. Durnell* does one thing: it tells a man with cancer that the federal government's silence is now his problem. Justice Kavanaugh's 7-2 majority holds that because EPA signed off on Roundup's label without a cancer warning, Missouri cannot make Bayer add one. Any such state-law duty would impose requirements "in addition to or different from" FIFRA's, and so it is expressly preempted. Clean text, clean result. I think it is the wrong call.

## The Bates problem

The majority's biggest obstacle is a precedent it never really tames: *Bates v. Dow Agrosciences* (2005), which held that FIFRA does **not** preempt failure-to-warn claims that merely parallel FIFRA's own ban on misbranding. A jury asking "was this label adequate?" is enforcing the standard Congress already wrote, not a different one. Kavanaugh distinguishes *Bates* by treating EPA's approval as an affirmative federal determination that no warning was required. But registration has never been a safety adjudication. It is a paperwork decision built largely on the manufacturer's own data, and the agency itself has wobbled on glyphosate for years. Converting that bureaucratic shrug into a preemptive federal command is precisely the move *Bates* warned against.

## What this favors

This is a structural win for every pesticide registrant in the country. The label becomes a liability shield: persuade EPA to bless an omission, and the omission turns bulletproof in all fifty states. The decision favors agency-approved silence over jury-tested accountability, and it nationalizes one regulator's risk tolerance even where that regulator never weighed the tort question at all. Jackson, joined tellingly by Gorsuch, sees the stakes plainly — the holding leaves genuinely injured plaintiffs with no remedy anywhere.

## The aftermath

Expect the tens of thousands of pending Roundup claims to be reframed overnight, as defense counsel wave this opinion at every warning count tied to an EPA-approved label. Power now shifts decisively to EPA itself: whoever controls the agency controls the warning, and therefore the liability. That should unsettle anyone who has watched registration science get politicized. Congress could fix this with a single clarifying sentence that approval is not preemption. It won't.

## My take

Thomas's concurrence and the majority's textualism are not frivolous; the preemption clause's words do cut their way. But law that is plausible on the page can still be unjust in the world. By turning a permissive federal label into an absolute defense, the Court has handed manufacturers a tidy way to launder dangerous silence through a compliant agency. *Durnell* is neat doctrine and a bad outcome. The dissent has the better of it.
