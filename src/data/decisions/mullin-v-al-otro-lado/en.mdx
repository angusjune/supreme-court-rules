## What the ruling does

This is a decision that turns a humanitarian statute on the placement of a single foot. By a 6-3 margin, the Court held that an asylum seeker standing at the U.S.-Mexico line — on the bridge, in the queue, a yard short of American pavement — has not "arrived in the United States" and so is owed nothing: no inspection, no chance to say the words "I am afraid to go back." Justice Alito's reasoning is disarmingly simple. "No one," he writes, "would say that a person 'arrives in' a place before the person enters that place." Tidy. And, I think, wrong in a way that matters more than its tidiness lets on.

Textualism is supposed to be the discipline of reading words in their full statutory home, not pulling one verb out and pressing it like a flower. Congress built the asylum regime atop the 1967 Protocol and the principle of non-refoulement — the promise that we will not push the persecuted back into harm. Read against that architecture, "arrives" describes the act of presenting oneself at our threshold, which is exactly what these migrants did. The majority's grammar lesson answers a question no one was really asking.

## What this favors

Power, and the executive's appetite for it. "Metering" — the bureaucratic euphemism for stationing officers on the international boundary to keep asylum seekers physically outside — is now blessed as policy rather than evasion. DHS gains a switch it can flip to make the asylum right disappear, not by repealing it but by never letting anyone reach it. It rewards geography over need and procedure over the very people the procedure was written to protect.

## The aftermath

Expect the line to move south. With legal turnbacks ratified, shelters in Tijuana and Matamoros will swell, smuggling networks will quote higher prices for the desperate, and more people will gamble on the desert and the river instead of the port of entry. Justice Sotomayor's warning that "more people will die" is not rhetoric; it is the predictable arithmetic of closing the lawful door. Litigation will now shift to where, exactly, "U.S. soil" begins — a grim cartography of inches.

## My take

The Court mistook a narrow reading for a neutral one. Choosing the cramped meaning of "arrives" was a choice, and it landed squarely against the powerless. A statute meant to catch the persecuted has been told it may only do so once the persecuted have already slipped past the guards posted to stop them. That is not textualism. That is a locked door with a sign that reads "walk in." Wrongly decided, and people will pay for it.
