## What the ruling does

Three words did the work here: "any determination." On that slender textual hook, a six-Justice majority pulled the courthouse door shut on roughly 350,000 Haitians and several thousand Syrians who had built lives under Temporary Protected Status. Justice Alito's opinion treats the review-bar as a clear, broad command — so broad that the ordinary tools of administrative law, the Administrative Procedure Act chief among them, simply do not reach a Secretary's decision to end protection. This was the wrong call, and not a close one in its consequences.

## What this favors

The decision is a gift to executive power and a blow to the idea that agencies must explain themselves. By converting a jurisdictional bar into a near-total immunity, the Court tells DHS that the *reasons* for a termination are nobody's business but its own. Pretext, sloppiness, even bad faith become functionally costless, so long as the action is dressed up as a "determination" and stops short of a naked constitutional violation. That favors speed and unaccountability over deliberation. It rewards an administration that wanted these terminations to be unreviewable precisely because they could not survive review.

The majority's treatment of the equal-protection claim compounds the problem. Waving it away as unlikely to succeed — in a posture about preliminary relief, no less — lets the Court avoid the uncomfortable record while still greenlighting the removals.

## The aftermath

Expect the protections to lapse on the government's timetable, with hundreds of thousands pushed toward undocumented status, work-authorization cliffs, and removal proceedings. Mixed-status families and the employers who depend on these workers absorb the shock. Litigants will now funnel everything into the one channel the majority left open — constitutional claims — which means more strained equal-protection and due-process theories chasing relief that statutory review used to provide cleanly. And every future Secretary, of either party, inherits a precedent that insulates immigration determinations from second-guessing.

## My take

Justice Kagan has the better of it. A statute that bars review of "any determination" should not be read to bless determinations that are arbitrary, discriminatory, or unexplained — unreviewable power is exactly the kind a careful court should hesitate to find. The majority chose textual tidiness over the messier duty of keeping the executive honest. The result is administrable, sure. It is also a quiet abdication, and the people who will pay for it are the ones least able to. Wrongly decided, and we will feel it.
