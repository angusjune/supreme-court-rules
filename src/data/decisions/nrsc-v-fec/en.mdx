## What the ruling does

Another wall around money in politics comes down. Justice Kavanaugh, for the six conservatives, struck down the federal limits on how much a political party can spend in coordination with its own candidates, holding that the caps violate the First Amendment. To get there, the Court walked away from *Colorado II*, its own 2001 precedent that upheld exactly these limits. Parties may now coordinate spending with their nominees without a ceiling — months before a midterm. The case carries Vice President JD Vance's fingerprints from his 2022 Senate run, when he wanted the NRSC's deeper pockets behind fully coordinated ads. He, and every well-financed party committee, just got the rule they asked for.

## What this favors

This favors the parties and, more precisely, the candidates who can now route party money into coordinated messaging without limit. The majority frames coordinated party spending as core First Amendment activity — speech and association at the heart of how parties support nominees — and insists the new rule "treats all political parties equally." Formally true. Functionally, it favors whoever can raise the most, and it weakens one of the last structural checks separating party money from candidate control. The doctrine strengthened here is the *Buckley* line's steady expansion: more and more of campaign finance reframed as speech, fewer and fewer limits surviving scrutiny. The anti-circumvention rationale that held coordinated limits together for a quarter century is the casualty.

## The aftermath

Expect money to move fast. Coordinated party expenditures had been capped in the low six figures for House races and up to a few million for Senate races; that ceiling is gone for the 2026 cycle. Donors who max out to a candidate can now give more to the party with the realistic expectation it will be spent in coordination with that same candidate — the precise circumvention the limits were built to prevent. The FEC is left enforcing a thinner rulebook, and reform advocates will pivot to disclosure, since contribution caps keep losing at the Court. The dissenters' fear is concrete: the practical line between a contribution and a coordinated expenditure was the whole point, and it just got a lot blurrier.

## My take

This is a results-driven retreat dressed as First Amendment principle. *Colorado II* was not some doctrinal orphan; it was a considered judgment that coordinated party spending functions as a conduit around contribution limits the Court has repeatedly upheld. Overruling it does not "treat all parties equally" so much as treat all parties as equally unleashed — which, in a system where one side always has the bigger war chest at a given moment, is not neutrality, it is advantage. I see the speech interest; I do not see why it required erasing a guardrail that kept candidate-contribution limits from becoming a formality. Verdict: a real constitutional argument pushed past where the anti-corruption interest should have stopped it, and the midterms will be the first stress test.
