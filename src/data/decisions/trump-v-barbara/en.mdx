## What the ruling does

For 127 years the rule has been simple: if you are born here, you are a citizen. On the last day of its term, the Supreme Court declined to unwrite it. Chief Justice Roberts, joined by Justices Barrett, Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson, affirmed the order blocking Executive Order 14160 and held that the Fourteenth Amendment means what it has always been understood to mean. A child born on American soil is "subject to the jurisdiction" of the United States, full stop, whatever the immigration status of the parents. This was the right call, and on the text and the history it was not the close case the administration wanted everyone to believe it was.

## What this favors

This favors the constitutional text over executive fiat, and it favors roughly every American who has ever taken a birth certificate to be proof of citizenship. The majority leans on the obvious sources — the 1866 debates, the Citizenship Clause's deliberate repudiation of *Dred Scott*, and *United States v. Wong Kim Ark*, the 1898 case that already answered this question for the child of noncitizen parents. Notice the coalition: this was not the liberals dragging a reluctant Court. Barrett joined the Chief, and Kavanaugh agreed the order was unlawful even as he reached the result through the immigration statutes rather than the Constitution. When five Justices say a thing is unconstitutional and a sixth says it is illegal anyway, the policy is dead twice over.

## The aftermath

The order is enjoined nationwide, so no hospital, no passport office, and no state vital-records bureau has to act on it. The children swept up in the litigation — including the infant known in the caption only as "Barbara" — are citizens, as they always were. The dissent from Thomas, joined by Gorsuch, reframes the history and signals that a determined litigant might keep probing the edges, but the core holding is now precedent with a clear six-vote majority behind it. Expect the fight over immigration to move back to where it belongs: Congress, and the political branches, not a unilateral redefinition of who counts as American.

## My take

This is the Court at its most defensible — refusing an invitation to rewrite a foundational guarantee by executive order, and doing it with a cross-ideological majority that makes the result hard to dismiss as partisan. Citizenship is not a privilege the President hands out and takes back; it is the floor the Fourteenth Amendment poured after a civil war fought partly over who belongs. Roberts said as much, and he was right to. The promise held. That it ever needed defending is the more troubling story.
