## What the ruling does

Give a President the power to fire a Federal Reserve governor at will, on a pretext, and you have handed the White House a dial marked "interest rates." A 5-4 Supreme Court just refused to do that. Chief Justice Roberts, joined by Justice Kavanaugh and the Court's three liberals, denied the Government's stay and left Lisa Cook in her seat. The reasoning is almost boringly correct: a "for cause" tenure is not at-will employment, "cause" has to mean something, and a court is allowed to check whether the asserted cause is real. This was the right call, and it was never the close question its packaging suggested.

## What this favors

Three things win here. First, central-bank independence — the century-old understanding that monetary policy should not swing with the President's reelection calendar. Second, judicial review: the majority rejects the theory that "cause" means whatever the President later says it means. Third, ordinary due process — Cook was entitled to notice and a chance to respond before being pushed out over an unproven mortgage-fraud allegation. Notice how modest each holding is. The Court did not resurrect a muscular *Humphrey's Executor*; it shielded the Fed because the Fed is *sui generis*. Read the fine print and you can see the majority quietly conceding the President more room to remove the heads of other independent agencies.

## The aftermath

Cook keeps voting on rates while her case proceeds, and markets exhaled at the signal that the FOMC will not be reshuffled by press release. But this is a stay, not a final word. The merits still must be litigated, and the broader war over presidential removal power — the one that actually threatens the FTC, the NLRB, and the rest of the alphabet — is merely deferred. Expect the administration to keep the pressure on the Fed through other channels, and expect the next removal case to test exactly how special the Court thinks the Fed really is.

## My take

This is a good day for the rule of law, and a narrow one. The Court drew the single brightest line it could not afford to erase — the Fed — and left much of the surrounding terrain negotiable. Roberts protected the one institution most likely to trigger a market panic if politicized, and he did it with a coalition that tells you the stakes were obvious even to the Court's center-right. Right outcome, sound reasoning, and a quietly ominous footnote for every other watchdog that lacks the Fed's mystique. Cook stays. The fight moves on.
