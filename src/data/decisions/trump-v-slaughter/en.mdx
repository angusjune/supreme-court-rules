## What the ruling does

For ninety-one years, *Humphrey's Executor* let Congress build agencies whose leaders the President could not fire at whim. As of June 29, that world is gone. In *Trump v. Slaughter*, a 6-3 Court led by Chief Justice Roberts ruled that the FTC's commissioners wield core executive power, that Article II vests all of that power in one elected President, and that a statute shielding them from removal except for "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance" cannot stand. Rebecca Slaughter, fired by President Trump and once reinstated by a district court, is out for good. The majority did not trim the 1935 precedent. It buried it.

## What this favors

This favors the presidency, full stop. The decision hands whoever occupies the Oval Office a clean line of command over the FTC and, by the logic of the opinion, over every multimember "independent" body built on the same removal protection: the FCC, the SEC, the NLRB, the FEC. Power that Congress deliberately diffused across bipartisan commissions now runs up a single chain to a single person. It favors a vision of accountability in which the only check that counts is the ballot box every four years, and it rewards the litigants and the White House that spent a decade hunting for the vehicle to retire *Humphrey's*. They found it.

## The aftermath

Expect a scramble. Commissioners at independent agencies now serve at presidential pleasure, which means partisan turnover the instant an administration changes, and it means market regulators, election watchdogs, and labor referees may hesitate before crossing a President who can dismiss them tomorrow. Sotomayor, joined by Kagan and Jackson, warns in dissent that the ruling dismantles a century of institutional design, and she is not exaggerating. Gorsuch, concurring, wanted the Court to go further still, which tells you the ground has not stopped moving. The Federal Reserve was carefully flagged as possibly different; that carve-out will be litigated hard, because everyone understands what is at stake if it does not hold.

## My take

The formalist argument is not frivolous. If these officials exercise executive power, the constitutional text is hard to read around, and *Humphrey's* always sat awkwardly with it. But law is also about consequences, and this one trades the messy virtue of insulated expertise for the tidy danger of concentrated control. Independence was the feature, not the bug. The Court had narrower paths available -- distinguish, cabin, leave the precedent wounded but standing. It chose demolition. A President who can fire every regulator who displeases him is more powerful, but the public those regulators were built to protect is not safer. This was a victory for neatness in constitutional theory and a defeat for the diffusion of power that keeps any single officeholder honest. History, I suspect, will not be kind.
