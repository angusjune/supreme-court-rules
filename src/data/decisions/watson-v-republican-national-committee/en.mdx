## What the ruling does

The Court read three terse federal statutes — the ones fixing a single national Election Day — and refused to torture them into a nationwide ban on counting timely-mailed ballots. Barrett's majority says the obvious thing out loud: an election is "held" on the day voters vote, and a mailed ballot is "cast" the moment it leaves the voter's hands, not whenever a clerk happens to slit the envelope. Mississippi's five-business-day grace window for ballots postmarked by Election Day survives. The Fifth Circuit's contrary preemption holding is reversed. This was the right call, and on the text it was not close.

## What this favors

This favors voters over gotcha litigation, and federalism over a manufactured uniformity. The Elections Clause hands states the pen on the "Manner" of elections; Congress set a day, not a delivery deadline. Roughly 30 states — red, blue, and purple — count ballots that land after Election Day so long as they were postmarked in time. Military and overseas voters, rural voters, anyone at the mercy of the Postal Service, all keep their ballots. The decision also rewards honest textualism: Barrett, joined by Roberts and the three Democratic appointees, declined to read a receipt deadline into statutes that say nothing about receipt.

## The aftermath

Watch the coalition, because it is the tell. Alito, Thomas, and Gorsuch — with Kavanaugh along for most of the ride — wanted to invalidate a practice that predates this controversy by decades, on the theory that "held" secretly means "received and counted." That reading would have thrown election administration into chaos months before the 2026 midterms and disenfranchised voters in dozens of states through no fault of their own. The remand sends the case back, but the preemption theory is dead. Expect the fight to shift to state legislatures, where opponents of late-arriving ballots will now have to win the argument politically instead of importing it through the federal courts.

## My take

This is the rare election case where the Court did less and got more right. The dissent dressed up a policy preference — everything must be in the building by Tuesday — as a command of statutory text, and four Justices bought it. Barrett did not, and her opinion is the better lawyering. A ballot dropped in the mail on Election Day is cast on Election Day; the rest is logistics. Conservatives who claim to revere the Elections Clause and state authority should be cheering a ruling that leaves the "Manner" of elections where the Constitution put it — with the states. Verdict: correct, careful, and a quiet rebuke to the notion that "textualism" means whatever outcome a litigant happens to want this week.
