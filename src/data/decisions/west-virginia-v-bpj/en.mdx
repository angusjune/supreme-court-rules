## What the ruling does

The Court handed the States a broad green light. Justice Kavanaugh, for all six conservatives, reversed two courts of appeals and upheld West Virginia's and Idaho's laws barring transgender girls from girls' school teams. The holding is sweeping in its framing — States "may determine eligibility for women's and girls' sports based on biological sex" — and it resolves under both Title IX and the Constitution at once. Becky Pepper-Jackson, who has competed on girls' teams since elementary school, and Lindsay Hecox, a college runner, lose. So, in practical terms, does every transgender student-athlete in a State inclined to write the same rule.

## What this favors

This favors the States' regulatory hand and the political movement that has made school sports a front line. The majority frames the laws as protecting competitive opportunity for biological females — the same equal-opportunity rationale that built Title IX in the first place — and that framing does real work. But read the alignment carefully: even the three dissenters did not adopt the plaintiffs' broad Title IX theory, which means the live fight was always the Equal Protection Clause, and there the Court split cleanly 6-3 along ideological lines. What is strengthened is the principle that classifying by "biological sex" in athletics survives constitutional scrutiny. What is weakened is the proposition, advanced below, that transgender status triggers the heightened scrutiny courts give sex-based classifications.

## The aftermath

Roughly half the States already have laws like these; this decision insulates them and clears the runway for the rest. Expect a wave of enforcement and a parallel wave of hard cases the opinion does not cleanly answer — what counts as "biological sex" for eligibility, how it is verified, and how the holding interacts with the Court's own recent gender-identity precedents. The dissenters' warning is the one to watch: by declining to treat transgender students as a protected class for equal-protection purposes, the Court leaves them more exposed across other domains, not just the playing field. This was a sports case on its face and a much larger doctrinal marker underneath.

## My take

I think the majority reached too far for too little. There was a narrow path here — defer to States on the genuinely contested science of competitive advantage in specific sports and age brackets — and the Court instead announced a categorical rule that treats a transgender girl who has lived as a girl for a decade as interchangeable, for eligibility purposes, with any boy. The equal-opportunity rationale is real, but it cuts in more than one direction, and the human cost lands on a tiny number of kids who mostly just want to run. Defensible on the statute, in my view harder to defend on the Constitution. The dissent has the better of the equal-protection argument, and we will be litigating its consequences for years.
