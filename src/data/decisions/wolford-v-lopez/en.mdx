## The decision, and why it lands wrong

Hawaii tried to do something modest and sensible: it told permit holders that a coffee shop, a hardware store, or a strip mall is presumptively a no-gun zone unless the owner says otherwise. Walk in armed only where you are welcome to. The Court's 6-3 majority, speaking through Justice Alito, calls that a constitutional affront — a rule that "hobbles" the right to carry as Americans go about their daily lives. I think the majority got the framing exactly backwards, and the consequences will be felt far beyond Honolulu.

The sleight of hand is the default. Hawaii did not ban carry on private property; it simply flipped the presumption. The state asked owners to opt **in** to armed patrons rather than forcing the public to track which of a thousand storefronts has opted **out**. Justice Jackson's dissent nails the point: this was always a property-rights question, and there is no constitutional right to march onto someone else's land armed and uninvited. Justice Kagan's separate dissent supplies the history the majority waves away — colonial and founding-era consent statutes that did precisely what Hawaii did.

## What this favors

This ruling favors the armed individual over the property owner and the community that owner serves. By treating a default rule as a "severe restriction," the majority quietly converts the Second Amendment into an easement across every business open to the public. Bruen promised a text-and-history test; in practice the majority deployed it as a one-way ratchet, demanding a perfect historical twin for any regulation it dislikes while ignoring the analogues that cut against it.

## The aftermath

The damage is not confined to Hawaii. California, Maryland, New York, and New Jersey built comparable default-no-carry frameworks after Bruen, and each is now on life support. Expect a wave of copycat suits, hurried legislative rewrites shifting the burden back onto owners, and a patchwork in which a small-business proprietor must post conspicuous signage or be deemed to have consented to firearms on the premises. That is more litigation, not less, and more confusion for the people actually standing behind the counter.

## My take

This was a wrong and avoidable decision. A presumption is not a prohibition, and a state that lets owners decide who comes armed onto their own land is protecting liberty, not crushing it. The majority mistook a default for a ban and a policy choice for a constitutional violation. The verdict: a doctrinally thin opinion that hands gun-rights advocates a sweeping win while leaving property owners holding the bag — and reads history selectively to get there.
