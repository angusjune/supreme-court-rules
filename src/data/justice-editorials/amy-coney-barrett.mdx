## The record

Barrett is the most intellectually disciplined conservative on the Roberts
Court, and this term proved that discipline cuts in more than one direction. She
authored the 6-3 majority in *Cisco Systems v. Doe*, slamming the door on new
causes of action under the Alien Tort Statute and on aiding-and-abetting
liability -- a quiet but sweeping narrowing of human-rights litigation that
reads like a Scalia footnote grown into a holding. She supplied a vote to
overrule *Humphrey's Executor* in *Trump v. Slaughter*, freeing the President to
remove FTC commissioners. On paper, a banner conservative term.

Then there is *Watson v. Republican National Committee*. Barrett wrote the 5-4
majority -- joined by Chief Justice Roberts and all three liberals -- holding
that the federal Election Day statutes do not preempt Mississippi's rule
counting absentee ballots postmarked by Election Day. The RNC lost. The textual
argument that would have helped her own side simply did not survive her reading
of the statute. That tension is the whole point of Barrett.

## Where she's pushing the law

Her signature is procedure: standing, remedies, and who gets to decide. Last
term's *Trump v. CASA* -- her majority reining in nationwide "universal"
injunctions -- was the most consequential remedies ruling in a generation, and
it runs in a straight line from her unanimous standing opinion in *FDA v.
Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine*. Barrett keeps asking the question the other
conservatives sometimes skip: not who should win, but whether the court is even
allowed to grant the relief sought.

She is no libertarian, though. She dissented in *Chatrie v. United States*,
rejecting the majority's view that a geofence warrant was a Fourth Amendment
search, and dissented again in *Trump v. Cook*, where she would have let the
President remove a Federal Reserve governor. Her *Wolford v. Lopez* concurrence
pressed the majority to anchor its Second Amendment result in history rather
than instinct. Method first, outcome second.

## My take

Barrett is now the Court's center of gravity, and that is a function of rigor,
not moderation. She votes conservative far more often than not -- but she is the
one justice on the right whose vote cannot be assumed once the statute is on the
table, and the only one regularly able to assemble a cross-ideological majority.
The left mistakes her *CASA* and *Cisco* opinions for partisanship; the right
grumbles every time she writes that the text "doesn't say that." Both reactions
are evidence she is doing the job as she sees it. Watch the remedies cases --
that is where Barrett, more than anyone on this bench, is quietly rewriting what
federal courts are permitted to do.
