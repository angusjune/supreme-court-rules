## The record

If you want to know where this Supreme Court will actually land, watch Brett
Kavanaugh's hands, not his rhetoric. In the latest run of decisions he was, as
usual, almost everywhere the majority was. He joined Alito to wave through the
administration's immigration agenda in *Mullin v. Doe* and *Mullin v. Al Otro
Lado*, joined the Second Amendment expansion in *Wolford v. Lopez*, and joined
Gorsuch in *Landor v. Louisiana Department of Corrections* to shut the
courthouse door on a Rastafarian inmate whose head was shaved in violation of
his faith. This is a conservative vote, full stop.

But Kavanaugh is also the justice who keeps a foot on the brake. In *Chatrie v.
United States* he crossed over to Kagan's majority, extending *Carpenter* to
hold that a Google "geofence" warrant is a Fourth Amendment search. And in
*Monsanto v. Durnell* he wrote the 7-2 opinion himself — a tidy
federal-preemption holding that reads less like a crusade than a statute being
applied.

## Where he's pushing the law

The tell is removal power. Kavanaugh joined *Trump v. Slaughter*, the 6-3
decision that finally buried *Humphrey's Executor* and stripped for-cause
protection from the FTC — a genuine earthquake in administrative law. Yet days
later, in *Trump v. Cook*, he joined the majority refusing to let the President
fire Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook, and wrote separately to explain why the
Fed is different. That is his whole method in miniature: tear down the old
structure, then draw the line that keeps the demolition from reaching the
building he wants to save.

You see the same move in his broader output. He authored *Kennedy v. Braidwood
Management* (2025), saving the ACA's preventive-care mandate on the theory that
the task force's members are removable inferior officers — structure, not
ideology. In *Trump v. CASA* he signed onto curbing nationwide injunctions but
concurred to flag the off-ramps: class actions, APA vacatur, the Court's own
emergency docket.

## My take

Kavanaugh is the most consequential justice almost nobody quotes. He rarely
writes the ringing dissent or the landmark majority; he writes the concurrence
that quietly becomes the real holding, the sentence that tells lower courts how
far is too far. Calling him a "moderate" misses it — he votes with the
conservative bloc relentlessly. He is better understood as the Court's governor,
the man who sets the *speed* of the revolution. The danger is that
incrementalism can launder radicalism: a doctrine demolished "narrowly" is still
demolished. But if you want to predict this Court, stop reading Thomas and start
reading Kavanaugh's separate writings. The center of gravity sits exactly where
he decides to stand.
