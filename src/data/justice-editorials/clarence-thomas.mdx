## The record

For most of three decades, Clarence Thomas was the Court's voice in the
wilderness, filing solo opinions almost nobody joined and fewer still expected
to matter. That era is over. This term he is not a dissenter pleading with the
future; he is a member of working conservative majorities reshaping the present.
Look at the run of June 2026 decisions. He joined the six-justice majority in
*Trump v. Slaughter* that buried *Humphrey's Executor* and the independent
agency it protected for ninety-one years. He was in the *Wolford v. Lopez*
majority extending *Bruen*'s text-and-history method to strike Hawaii's
default ban on carrying firearms onto private property. He signed the majorities
narrowing the Alien Tort Statute in *Cisco v. Doe* and slamming the asylum door
shut in *Mullin v. Al Otro Lado*.

## Where they're pushing the law

What makes Thomas distinctive is that joining is never enough. In *Trump v.
Slaughter* he pointedly declined to sign Part III-B of the Chief's opinion,
signaling he would go further than even his allies on executive power. In
*Monsanto v. Durnell* he joined a 7-2 preemption holding and then wrote
separately anyway, unable to let the majority's reasoning stand uncorrected. And
when the conservatives lost, he was the one furthest right: dissenting in
*Trump v. Cook* to let the President remove a Federal Reserve governor at will,
dissenting in *Chatrie* against treating geofence warrants as Fourth Amendment
searches, and joining Alito to reject Mississippi's ballot grace period in
*Watson v. RNC*. The through-line is method, not party. Off the site's docket,
his 2025 *Skrmetti* concurrence repudiated medical deference, and his
*Louisiana v. Callais* concurrence argued Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act
does not regulate districting at all. He keeps planting flags where the rest of
the Court has not yet camped.

## My take

Thomas is the most consequential justice on the bench precisely because he is the
most patient. He has spent thirty-four years building an originalist case file
of solo concurrences, and his colleagues are now mining it for holdings. That is
real intellectual influence, and it deserves to be named as such rather than
dismissed. But influence cuts both ways. His method treats long-settled
precedent as merely a wrong answer awaiting correction, and a Court that follows
him will keep yanking the ground out from under people who relied on it, from
asylum seekers to agency independence. I admire the consistency and distrust the
destination. The wilderness years gave Thomas a coherence few justices ever
achieve. The question for the next decade is whether a man who was right to
doubt the consensus knows when to stop dynamiting it.
