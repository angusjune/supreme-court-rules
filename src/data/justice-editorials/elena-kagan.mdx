## The record

This term confirmed what has been true for a while: Kagan is the most consequential strategist on the Court's left. When the votes exist, she gets the gavel. In *Chatrie v. United States*, she wrote for a 6-3 majority holding that a Google "geofence" warrant is a Fourth Amendment search, stitching Roberts and Kavanaugh onto the three liberals and pulling Gorsuch into the judgment on his own property-and-positive-law terms. That is not luck; that is craft. She did it again, quietly, in *Trump v. Cook*, joining the Chief's five-Justice majority that kept Fed Governor Lisa Cook in her seat and walled the central bank off from at-will removal.

When she loses, she loses loudly and on purpose. She joined Sotomayor's dissent in *Trump v. Slaughter*, the decision that buried *Humphrey's Executor* after 91 years, and signed the dissents as the majority cleared the way for the administration in the *Mullin* immigration cases.

## Where she's pushing the law

Watch *Wolford v. Lopez*. The majority struck down Hawaii's default-no-carry rule under *Bruen*'s text-and-history test; Kagan wrote separately to do something sly — she handed the majority the colonial and founding-era consent statutes it pretended did not exist. This is her signature move now: take the conservatives' originalism at face value, then show that the history does not say what they want it to. She has spent years arguing the current Court is "textualist only when it suits it," and she has stopped merely complaining about it. She is building the counter-record, brick by brick, inside the majority's own method.

Her other through-line is institutional. Across the removal-power cases and her long war on the major-questions doctrine, Kagan defends the administrative state not as bureaucratic clutter but as democratic self-government — Congress's right to structure the government it created.

## My take

Kagan is playing a longer game than anyone else on the bench. She has accepted that she will lose the marquee separation-of-powers fights for now, so she writes dissents engineered to be quoted in 2040, and she banks the winnable cases — *Chatrie*, *Cook* — with a discipline her colleagues do not always show. The risk is real: coalition-building can read as caution, and a justice this good at the inside game can spend a career one vote short. But make no mistake — when this majority finally cracks, it will be Kagan's opinions, not the angriest ones, that supply the blueprint. She is the most effective liberal on this Court, and by far the most patient.
