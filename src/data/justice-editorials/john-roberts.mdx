## The record

Forget the balls-and-strikes routine. The defining fact about John Roberts this term is that he was almost never in dissent — by most counts in the majority well over ninety percent of the time — because the Chief Justice does not lose cases he cares about; he assigns them. Run the tape. He joined the conservative bloc to gut RLUIPA damages in *Landor*, to seal the Alien Tort Statute shut in *Cisco Systems v. Doe*, to preempt cancer-warning suits in *Monsanto v. Durnell*, to ratify border "metering" in *Mullin v. Al Otro Lado*, to make TPS terminations unreviewable in *Mullin v. Doe*, and to blow a hole in Hawaii's gun law in *Wolford v. Lopez*. That is not an umpire. That is a man with a thesis.

## Where he's pushing the law

The thesis is presidential power, and on June 29 he wrote it down. In *Trump v. Slaughter*, Roberts personally authored the demolition of *Humphrey's Executor* — the 1935 decision he had spent the better part of two decades chipping at — and handed every President a clean line of command over the FTC, and by implication the SEC, the FCC, the NLRB, the FEC. Ninety-one years of diffused, bipartisan-commission design, gone in one opinion under his own name.

Now read it next to what he did the *same day*. In *Trump v. Cook*, the very same Chief Justice peeled off Kavanaugh and the three liberals to keep Lisa Cook in her Federal Reserve seat, declaring the Fed *sui generis* and a President's "cause" something a court is allowed to second-guess. Demolition and carve-out, issued hours apart, both signed Roberts. That is the whole method in miniature: hand the executive the maximal principle, then quietly fence off the one institution — the central bank — whose politicization would trigger a market panic he'd own. The same instinct drove his vote in *Watson v. RNC*, where he sided with Barrett and the liberals to save late-arriving mail ballots in thirty states rather than light a fuse under the 2026 midterms.

## My take

Call it institutional self-preservation dressed as restraint. Roberts is not a moderate; he is a strategist who understands that a Court seen as torching the economy or the franchise loses the legitimacy he treasures, while a Court that merely re-engineers the separation of powers can do so behind the dignity of "text" and "structure." His genius — and it is genius — is knowing exactly which lines cannot be crossed in public. The trajectory is unmistakable: he has gotten nearly everything the conservative project wanted, and made it look like prudence. History will remember the demolition long after it forgets the carve-out.
