## The record

Ketanji Brown Jackson is, increasingly, the Court's great dissenter — and she has earned the title. This term she landed on the losing end again and again, and she rarely went quietly. She wrote the dissent in *Landor v. Louisiana Department of Corrections*, where a 6-3 majority told a Rastafarian inmate — forcibly shaved bald despite handing his guards a controlling precedent — that he held a right but no remedy. She wrote the dissent in *Wolford v. Lopez*, refusing to read the Second Amendment as a veto on a state's choice to keep guns off private property. And when the majority overruled *Humphrey's Executor* in *Trump v. Slaughter*, she joined Sotomayor in warning that a century of independent-agency design was being torn down. When she wins, it is often by joining a coalition the math demands: she signed Barrett's majority in *Watson v. RNC* to save Mississippi's late-arriving absentee ballots, and Roberts's in *Trump v. Cook* to keep a Federal Reserve governor in her seat.

## Where they're pushing the law

Watch the through-line: remedies. Jackson's animating question is not merely "what does the statute say" but "who is left with no way to enforce it." That is the heart of her *Landor* dissent, and of her real-world solo dissent in *Trump v. CASA*, where she warned that allowing the Executive to act unlawfully in some cases invites executive lawlessness to "flourish." It is also why her vote in *Monsanto v. Durnell* matters — a dissent she shared with Neil Gorsuch, of all people. She will cross the ideological aisle when a holding leaves genuinely injured plaintiffs with no remedy anywhere. In *Chatrie v. United States* she wrote separately to push Fourth Amendment privacy past the third-party fiction. The method is consistent even when the coalition is not.

## My take

Jackson is the most rhetorically fearless member of the Court, and that has both costs and benefits. Her solo dissents — long, urgent, occasionally scolding — draw the majority's irritation and the recurring charge that she argues from consequences rather than text. But the consequences are the point. A right without a remedy is a slogan, and Jackson is the justice least willing to pretend otherwise. She is not moving this Court's center; she is too junior and too outnumbered for that today. What she is doing is building the record — the dissent that a future Court, or a future Congress, eventually picks up. Bet on her influence growing not in this term's box scores but in the footnotes of the next decade. Verdict: the conscience of the Court's left, and its most quotable voice.
