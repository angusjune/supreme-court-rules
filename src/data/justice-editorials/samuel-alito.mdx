## The record

Across the term's marquee cases, Samuel Alito has been the conservative majority's workhorse pen — and its most predictable vote. He authored the immigration twins, *Mullin v. Doe* and *Mullin v. Al Otro Lado*, reading the phrase "any determination" to slam the courthouse door on roughly 350,000 Temporary Protected Status holders, then parsing the single verb "arrives" to leave asylum seekers stranded a foot short of American pavement. He wrote *Wolford v. Lopez*, wielding *Bruen*'s text-and-history test to strike Hawaii's default-no-carry rule for private property. And when he was not writing for the majority he was anchoring it, joining the demolition of *Humphrey's Executor* in *Trump v. Slaughter* and the gutting of the Alien Tort Statute in *Cisco Systems v. Doe*.

## Where they're pushing the law

Two engines drive Alito's jurisprudence, and they pull the same way. The first is a muscular executive. In *Trump v. Cook* he dissented, joined by Gorsuch, from the majority that kept the President's hands off the Federal Reserve — and by the logic of *Slaughter* he would happily strip removal protections from the rest of the independent agencies. The second is a textualism deployed with a thumb on the scale: generous when the government wants room, miserly when a migrant, a voter, or a prisoner needs it. See *Watson v. RNC*, where he dissented to read a ballot-receipt deadline into statutes that mention none, and *Landor*, where this self-styled champion of religious liberty joined an opinion that left a Rastafarian inmate forcibly shorn and remediless. His instincts on rights are selective — expansive in *Mahmoud v. Taylor*, his real-world June 2025 majority handing religious parents a strict-scrutiny opt-out, yet cramped when the claimant sits behind bars or waits at the border.

## My take

Alito is the most ideologically legible Justice on the Court — he and Thomas agreed in every closely divided case last term — and that legibility is both his strength and his tell. When he led the dissent in *Chatrie v. United States*, insisting that a sweeping geofence dragnet was no Fourth Amendment "search," he exposed the through-line: law enforcement and executive power get the benefit of the doubt, and the individual against the state rarely does. This is not the cautious incrementalist who once filled Sandra Day O'Connor's seat. It is a confident senior Justice who has stopped splitting differences. His method is genuine textualism, but it bends reliably toward power, and he no longer pretends otherwise. He is winning more than ever — and, by every account, seems to enjoy it less.
