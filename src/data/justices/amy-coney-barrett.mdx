## Background

Amy Vivian Coney Barrett was born on January 28, 1972, in New Orleans,
Louisiana, the eldest of seven children. She graduated magna cum laude from
Rhodes College in Memphis in 1994 with a degree in English literature, then
attended Notre Dame Law School on a full scholarship, earning her J.D. summa
cum laude in 1997. She finished first in her class and served as executive
editor of the Notre Dame Law Review.

After law school, Barrett clerked for Judge Laurence Silberman on the U.S.
Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit and then for Justice Antonin Scalia on
the Supreme Court during the 1998 term. She practiced law in Washington, D.C.,
before returning to Notre Dame Law School as a professor in 2002, where she
taught constitutional law, federal courts, and statutory interpretation and was
repeatedly recognized by students as a distinguished teacher.

In 2017, President Donald Trump appointed her to the U.S. Court of Appeals for
the Seventh Circuit, where the Senate confirmed her by a vote of 55-43. After
the death of Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg in September 2020, Trump nominated
Barrett to the Supreme Court. The Senate confirmed her on October 26, 2020, by
a vote of 52-48, and she took her seat the next day, becoming the fifth woman
to serve on the Court.

## Jurisprudence

Barrett describes herself as an originalist and textualist in the tradition of
her mentor, Justice Scalia, interpreting the Constitution according to its
original public meaning and statutes according to their text. She sits on the
conservative wing of the Court, but she has at times charted an independent
course, writing or joining separate opinions that part from the other
conservative justices on questions of standing, remedies, and the proper scope
of judicial decision-making. An academic by background, she tends to favor
narrow, incremental rulings and has shown particular interest in the limits of
Article III standing and in methodological questions about how courts should
read precedent and statutory text.

## Notable rulings

- **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022)** — Joined the majority
  overruling Roe v. Wade and Planned Parenthood v. Casey, returning the
  regulation of abortion to the states; her vote, filling Ginsburg's seat, was
  decisive.
- **Biden v. Nebraska (2023)** — Joined the majority striking down the
  administration's student-loan forgiveness program and wrote a concurrence
  defending the major questions doctrine as consistent with textualism.
- **Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023)** — Joined the majority
  holding that race-conscious college admissions programs violate the Equal
  Protection Clause and Title VI.
- **Trump v. United States (2024)** — Joined most of the majority recognizing
  presidential immunity for official acts but wrote separately, disagreeing with
  the holding that official-acts evidence cannot be introduced in a prosecution
  for unofficial conduct.
- **FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine (2024)** — Wrote the Court's
  unanimous opinion holding that the challengers lacked Article III standing to
  contest the FDA's approval and regulation of the abortion drug mifepristone.
