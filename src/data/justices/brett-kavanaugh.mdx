## Background

Brett Michael Kavanaugh was born on February 12, 1965, in Washington, D.C., and
was raised in Bethesda, Maryland. His mother, Martha Kavanaugh, was a public
school teacher who later became a Maryland state trial judge, and his father was
a lawyer and trade-association executive. He attended Georgetown Preparatory
School before earning a B.A. from Yale University in 1987 and a J.D. from Yale
Law School in 1990, where he was an editor of the *Yale Law Journal*.

After law school he clerked for Judge Walter Stapleton of the U.S. Court of
Appeals for the Third Circuit and Judge Alex Kozinski of the Ninth Circuit, and
then for Justice Anthony M. Kennedy on the Supreme Court during the 1993 Term. In
the 1990s he worked with Independent Counsel Kenneth Starr, helping to author the
report that led to President Clinton's impeachment, and he spent time in private
practice at the firm Kirkland & Ellis. He later served in the administration of
President George W. Bush as Associate White House Counsel and then as White House
Staff Secretary from 2003 to 2006.

In 2006 President Bush appointed Kavanaugh to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the
D.C. Circuit, often regarded as the nation's second-most important court, where
he sat for twelve years and compiled an extensive record on administrative law
and separation-of-powers questions. President Donald Trump nominated him to the
Supreme Court on July 9, 2018, to fill the seat of the retiring Justice Anthony
M. Kennedy. His confirmation became one of the most contentious in modern
history after Christine Blasey Ford accused him of sexual assault during their
high school years, allegations he categorically denied at a nationally televised
hearing. The Senate confirmed him by a vote of 50-48 on October 6, 2018, and he
took his seat that same day.

## Jurisprudence

Kavanaugh is a member of the Court's six-justice conservative majority, but he is
frequently described as occupying a position toward its center, alongside Chief
Justice Roberts and Justice Barrett. His method draws on textualism and
originalism, yet he often favors narrow, incremental rulings, shows attention to
precedent and stare decisis, and is inclined to decide cases on the most limited
grounds available. He votes with the majority more often than almost any other
justice and regularly writes separate concurrences that map out where he believes
the law should and should not go. His record reflects particular interest in
separation of powers, administrative-agency authority, and the scope of executive
power, subjects he engaged closely during his years on the D.C. Circuit.

## Notable rulings

- **Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)** — Dissented from the majority holding that
  Title VII's bar on sex discrimination protects gay and transgender employees,
  arguing the question should have been left to Congress while acknowledging the
  result as an important victory for gay and lesbian Americans.
- **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022)** — Joined the majority
  overruling *Roe v. Wade* and wrote an influential concurrence describing the
  Constitution as neutral on abortion and framing the decision as returning the
  issue to the states and the democratic process.
- **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen (2022)** — Joined the
  majority adopting a history-and-tradition test for the Second Amendment and
  wrote a concurrence, joined by Chief Justice Roberts, stressing that
  "shall-issue" licensing regimes remain constitutionally permissible.
- **Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023)** — Joined the majority holding
  that race-conscious admissions programs at Harvard and the University of North
  Carolina violate the Equal Protection Clause, and wrote separately on the
  decision's place in the Court's equal-protection precedents.
- **Trump v. United States (2024)** — Joined the majority opinion recognizing that
  former presidents are entitled to substantial immunity from criminal
  prosecution for their official acts.
