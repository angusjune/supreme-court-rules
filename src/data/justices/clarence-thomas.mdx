## Background

Clarence Thomas was born on June 23, 1948, in the small community of Pin Point,
Georgia, near Savannah. After his father left the family, he was raised largely by
his maternal grandfather in Savannah and educated in Catholic schools. He briefly
studied for the priesthood, attending Conception Seminary College, before earning
his undergraduate degree from the College of the Holy Cross in 1971 and his law
degree from Yale Law School in 1974.

Thomas began his legal career as an assistant attorney general in Missouri under
then-Attorney General John Danforth, later working in private practice for Monsanto
and as a legislative aide to Danforth in the U.S. Senate. During the Reagan
administration he served as Assistant Secretary for Civil Rights at the Department
of Education and then as Chairman of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
(EEOC) from 1982 to 1990. President George H. W. Bush appointed him to the U.S.
Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit in 1990.

In 1991, Bush nominated Thomas to the Supreme Court to fill the seat of the
retiring Thurgood Marshall. His confirmation hearings became among the most
contentious in modern history after Anita Hill's testimony, and the Senate
confirmed him by a vote of 52-48. He took his seat on October 23, 1991, becoming
the second African American to serve on the Court. He is now its longest-serving
member and senior associate justice.

## Jurisprudence

Thomas is one of the Court's most committed originalists and textualists,
interpreting the Constitution according to what he understands to be its
original public meaning. He is notable among the justices for his willingness to
question and, where he believes the text requires it, to call for overruling
long-standing precedent that he regards as wrongly decided. He frequently writes
solo concurrences and dissents that lay out distinctive positions, including
skepticism toward substantive due process, interest in reviving the Privileges or
Immunities Clause, a narrow reading of the Commerce Clause, and a robust view of
the Second Amendment. Anchoring the right flank of the Court, he was for years
known for rarely speaking at oral argument, though he became a more active
questioner after the Court adopted telephonic arguments in 2020.

## Notable rulings

- **District of Columbia v. Heller (2008)** — Joined the majority holding that the
  Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms.
- **Gonzales v. Raich (2005)** — Dissented, arguing the Commerce Clause did not
  authorize federal regulation of homegrown marijuana used for medical purposes.
- **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen (2022)** — Wrote the majority
  opinion establishing a "text, history, and tradition" standard for evaluating
  firearm regulations.
- **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022)** — Joined the majority
  overruling Roe v. Wade and authored a concurrence urging the Court to reconsider
  other substantive due process precedents.
- **Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023)** — Joined the majority ending
  race-conscious college admissions and wrote a lengthy concurrence on the Equal
  Protection Clause.
