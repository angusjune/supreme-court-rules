## Background

Elena Kagan was born on April 28, 1960, in New York City. She graduated from
Princeton University in 1981, earned a master's degree from Worcester College,
Oxford, in 1983, and received her law degree from Harvard Law School in 1986,
where she was supervising editor of the *Harvard Law Review*.

After law school she clerked for Judge Abner Mikva on the U.S. Court of Appeals
for the D.C. Circuit and then for Justice Thurgood Marshall on the Supreme Court.
She practiced briefly in private practice before joining the faculty of the
University of Chicago Law School. During the Clinton administration she served
as Associate White House Counsel and as Deputy Director of the Domestic Policy
Council.

Kagan returned to academia at Harvard Law School, where in 2003 she became the
first woman to serve as dean. In 2009 President Obama named her Solicitor General
of the United States, the first woman to hold that office. The following year,
Obama nominated her to the Supreme Court to fill the seat vacated by the
retiring Justice John Paul Stevens. The Senate confirmed her by a vote of 63-37,
and she took her seat on August 7, 2010. Having never served as a judge before
her appointment, she came to the Court directly from advocacy and academia.

## Jurisprudence

Kagan sits among the Court's liberal members and is widely regarded as one of its
most influential opinion writers, known for plain-spoken, accessible prose. She
is closely associated with careful statutory interpretation and has acknowledged
the dominance of textualism in modern interpretation, while frequently arguing
that text must be read in light of statutory purpose and context. In recent terms
she has emerged as a leading critic of doctrines the conservative majority has
used to limit administrative agencies, including the "major questions doctrine,"
and as a defender of stare decisis. Often working to build coalitions, she
nonetheless writes pointed dissents when in the minority.

## Notable rulings

- **Cooper v. Harris (2017)** — Wrote the majority opinion holding that two North
  Carolina congressional districts were unconstitutional racial gerrymanders,
  reinforcing limits on the use of race in redistricting.
- **Kisor v. Wilkie (2019)** — Authored the controlling opinion that declined to
  overrule *Auer* deference to agencies' interpretations of their own regulations
  while significantly narrowing when it applies.
- **Rucho v. Common Cause (2019)** — Wrote a forceful dissent arguing that federal
  courts can and should adjudicate claims of partisan gerrymandering, after the
  majority held such claims nonjusticiable.
- **West Virginia v. EPA (2022)** — Wrote the principal dissent criticizing the
  Court's reliance on the major questions doctrine to curb the EPA's authority
  to regulate power-plant emissions.
- **Biden v. Nebraska (2023)** — Authored the dissent objecting to the Court's
  decision striking down the administration's student-loan forgiveness program,
  on both standing and statutory-authority grounds.
