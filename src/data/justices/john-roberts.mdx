## Background

John Glover Roberts Jr. was born on January 27, 1955, in Buffalo, New York, and grew up in Long Beach, Indiana. He attended Harvard College, where he graduated summa cum laude in three years, and then Harvard Law School, where he was managing editor of the *Harvard Law Review* and graduated magna cum laude in 1979.

After law school, Roberts clerked for Judge Henry Friendly of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit and then for Associate Justice William Rehnquist during the 1980 Term. He served in the Reagan administration as a special assistant to the Attorney General and as associate counsel to the President, and later as Principal Deputy Solicitor General in the George H. W. Bush administration. In private practice at the firm Hogan & Hartson, Roberts built a leading Supreme Court and appellate practice, arguing dozens of cases before the Court.

In 2003 he was appointed to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit. President George W. Bush nominated him to the Supreme Court in 2005, initially to fill the seat of retiring Justice Sandra Day O'Connor. After Chief Justice William Rehnquist died in September 2005, Bush renominated Roberts for the center chair. The Senate confirmed him as the 17th Chief Justice of the United States by a vote of 78-22, and he took office on September 29, 2005.

## Jurisprudence

Roberts is generally regarded as a conservative jurist with an institutionalist streak. He favors incremental, narrowly drawn decisions over sweeping pronouncements, often emphasizing judicial minimalism and stare decisis. He has expressed concern for the Court's public legitimacy and its appearance of being above partisan politics, famously analogizing the judge's role to that of an umpire calling balls and strikes.

As Chief Justice, he sits at the Court's ideological center-right and frequently controls the assignment of majority opinions. While reliably conservative on many questions, he has at times provided a pivotal vote or authored controlling opinions that frustrated the right, particularly where he viewed broader rulings as threatening the Court's institutional standing. His jurisprudence reflects skepticism of race-conscious government policy, support for a robust view of executive and presidential structure, and a cautious approach to overturning precedent.

## Notable rulings

- **National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius (2012)** — Roberts wrote the controlling opinion upholding the Affordable Care Act's individual mandate as a valid exercise of Congress's taxing power, casting the decisive vote to preserve the law.
- **Shelby County v. Holder (2013)** — He authored the majority opinion striking down the coverage formula in Section 4 of the Voting Rights Act, reshaping federal oversight of state election laws.
- **Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023)** — Roberts wrote the majority opinion holding that race-conscious admissions programs at Harvard and the University of North Carolina violated the Equal Protection Clause.
- **Trump v. United States (2024)** — He authored the majority opinion recognizing substantial presidential immunity from criminal prosecution for official acts, a landmark ruling on executive power.
- **Department of Commerce v. New York (2019)** — Roberts wrote the controlling opinion blocking the addition of a citizenship question to the 2020 census, finding the stated rationale to be pretextual.
