## Background

Ketanji Onyika Brown Jackson was born on September 14, 1970, in Washington, D.C., and raised in Miami, Florida. Both of her parents began their careers as public school teachers; her father later became an attorney for the Miami-Dade County School Board, and her mother served as a school principal. She has often credited those early years watching her father study law with sparking her own interest in the profession. At Miami Palmetto Senior High School she was a nationally successful competitive debater and was elected student body president.

She attended Harvard University, earning an A.B. in government magna cum laude in 1992, and then Harvard Law School, where she received her J.D. cum laude in 1996 and served as an editor of the Harvard Law Review. After law school she held a series of prestigious clerkships, working for Judge Patti Saris of the U.S. District Court in Massachusetts, Judge Bruce Selya of the First Circuit, and ultimately Justice Stephen Breyer at the Supreme Court during the 1999-2000 term.

Her professional path was unusually varied for a future justice. She worked in private practice and, notably, served from 2005 to 2007 as an Assistant Federal Public Defender in Washington, D.C., representing indigent defendants on appeal. She later served as Vice Chair and a Commissioner of the U.S. Sentencing Commission. In 2013, President Barack Obama appointed her to the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia, where she sat as a trial judge until 2021. President Joseph R. Biden then elevated her to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit, where she was confirmed in June 2021.

On February 25, 2022, President Biden nominated her to the Supreme Court to fill the seat of the retiring Justice Breyer, the justice for whom she had once clerked. The Senate confirmed her by a vote of 53-47 on April 7, 2022, and she took her seat on June 30, 2022, becoming the first Black woman and the first former federal public defender to serve on the Court.

## Jurisprudence

Jackson sits within the Court's three-member liberal wing, alongside Justices Sonia Sotomayor and Elena Kagan, and she is currently the most junior member of the Court. From her first term she established herself as an exceptionally active questioner at oral argument, frequently speaking more than any of her colleagues.

Her opinions often engage closely with constitutional text and history, including the original public meaning of the Reconstruction Amendments, while reaching conclusions different from those of the Court's originalist majority. She has shown a particular interest in questions of racial equality, criminal justice, administrative law, and the practical operation of legal rules, drawing on her experience as both a trial judge and a public defender. In the conservative-majority era she has become a frequent and pointed dissenter, often writing separately to spell out her disagreement.

## Notable rulings

- **Students for Fair Admissions v. University of North Carolina (2023)** — Wrote a dissent defending race-conscious admissions and grounding her argument in the history of race in America. She was recused from the companion case involving Harvard because of her prior service on its Board of Overseers, but participated in the UNC case.
- **Biden v. Nebraska (2023)** — Joined the dissent objecting to the majority's decision striking down the administration's student-loan forgiveness program and criticizing the Court's use of the "major questions" doctrine.
- **Trump v. Anderson (2024)** — Joined a concurrence in the judgment that agreed Colorado could not unilaterally bar Donald Trump from the ballot under Section 3 of the Fourteenth Amendment, while objecting that the majority decided more than the case required.
- **Trump v. United States (2024)** — Wrote her own dissent, in addition to joining the principal dissent, arguing that the majority's grant of broad criminal immunity placed a former president effectively above the law.
- **Relentless, Inc. v. Department of Commerce (2024)** — Joined the dissent from the Court's decision overruling Chevron deference, participating in this companion case after recusing from Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo because of her earlier involvement on the D.C. Circuit.
