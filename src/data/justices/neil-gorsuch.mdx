## Background

Neil McGill Gorsuch was born on August 29, 1967, in Denver, Colorado. His mother, Anne Gorsuch Burford, served as the first woman to head the Environmental Protection Agency under President Ronald Reagan, and the family moved to Washington, D.C., during her tenure. Gorsuch earned his bachelor's degree from Columbia University, a J.D. from Harvard Law School, and later a Doctor of Philosophy in legal philosophy from the University of Oxford, where he studied as a Marshall Scholar under the natural-law theorist John Finnis.

After law school he clerked for Judge David B. Sentelle of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit and then for Supreme Court Justices Byron White and Anthony Kennedy. He spent roughly a decade in private practice at the Washington firm Kellogg, Huber, Hansen, Todd, Evans & Figel, and served briefly as Principal Deputy Associate Attorney General in the U.S. Department of Justice.

In 2006, President George W. Bush appointed Gorsuch to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit, based in Denver, where he served for more than a decade and built a reputation as a clear, literary writer and a committed textualist. Following the death of Justice Antonin Scalia in 2016, President Donald Trump nominated Gorsuch to the Supreme Court on January 31, 2017. The Senate confirmed him by a vote of 54 to 45 after a change to its filibuster rules, and he took his seat on April 10, 2017, becoming the 113th Justice of the Supreme Court.

## Jurisprudence

Gorsuch is widely regarded as one of the Court's most committed textualists and originalists, frequently invoking the original public meaning of statutory and constitutional text. He often describes the judge's task as applying the words Congress actually enacted, even when the result cuts against expected ideological lines. He is an outspoken proponent of the separation of powers and has expressed deep skepticism of the modern administrative state, including doctrines that direct courts to defer to agency interpretations.

Among the conservative bloc, Gorsuch is notable for a distinctive libertarian and individual-rights streak, particularly on questions of criminal procedure, the rights of Native American tribes, and limits on government power. This has occasionally produced cross-ideological alliances and outcomes that surprise observers who expect a strict left-right divide. He is also a strong defender of religious-liberty claims and a critic of what he views as judicial improvisation untethered from text.

## Notable rulings

- **Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)** — Writing for a 6-3 majority, Gorsuch held that Title VII's prohibition on discrimination "because of sex" protects employees from being fired for being gay or transgender, a textualist reading that delivered a landmark victory for LGBTQ workers and surprised many conservatives.
- **McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020)** — Authoring the 5-4 majority, he ruled that much of eastern Oklahoma remains a Creek Nation reservation for purposes of federal criminal law, holding Congress to the promises in its treaties and reshaping jurisdiction across the region.
- **Carpenter v. United States (2018)** — He joined the majority requiring a warrant for historical cell-site location records and wrote a notable solo opinion grounding Fourth Amendment protection in property and the original understanding of the text.
- **West Virginia v. EPA (2022)** — He joined the majority limiting the EPA's authority to regulate power-plant emissions and wrote a concurrence elaborating the "major questions doctrine," reflecting his long-standing skepticism of broad agency power.
- **Sessions v. Dimaya (2018)** — He provided the decisive vote and concurred in striking down a vague immigration-removal provision, illustrating his willingness to side with the Court's liberals on due-process and vagueness grounds.
