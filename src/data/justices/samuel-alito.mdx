## Background

Samuel Anthony Alito Jr. was born on April 1, 1950, in Trenton, New Jersey, the
son of an Italian immigrant father who worked as a schoolteacher and later
directed New Jersey's Office of Legislative Services. He earned an A.B. from
Princeton University in 1972 and a J.D. from Yale Law School in 1975, where he
edited the *Yale Law Journal*.

After law school he clerked for Judge Leonard I. Garth of the U.S. Court of
Appeals for the Third Circuit. His government career was extensive: he served as
an Assistant U.S. Attorney for the District of New Jersey, then as an Assistant
to the Solicitor General, where he argued cases before the Supreme Court, and as
a Deputy Assistant Attorney General in the Office of Legal Counsel. From 1987 to
1990 he was the U.S. Attorney for the District of New Jersey. In 1990 President
George H.W. Bush appointed him to the Third Circuit, where he sat for fifteen
years and built a substantial record as an appellate judge.

President George W. Bush nominated Alito to the Supreme Court on October 31,
2005, to fill the seat of the retiring Justice Sandra Day O'Connor, after the
withdrawal of an earlier nominee. The Senate confirmed him by a vote of 58-42,
and he took his seat on January 31, 2006.

## Jurisprudence

Alito is generally regarded as one of the Court's most reliably conservative
members. His approach draws on originalism and textualism but is often described
as pragmatic and incremental, attentive to the concrete facts and consequences
of a case. He has shown particular concern for religious-liberty claims,
robust executive authority, and law-and-order considerations, and he frequently
votes to narrow precedents he views as wrongly decided. He is known for sharply
worded opinions and pointed questioning at oral argument. On the current Court he
sits firmly on the right alongside Justices Thomas and Gorsuch.

## Notable rulings

- **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022)** — authored the majority
  opinion overturning *Roe v. Wade* and *Planned Parenthood v. Casey*, holding that
  the Constitution confers no right to abortion and returning the issue to the states.
- **Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores (2014)** — wrote the majority opinion holding that
  closely held corporations could claim a religious exemption from the Affordable Care
  Act's contraceptive-coverage mandate under the Religious Freedom Restoration Act.
- **Janus v. AFSCME (2018)** — authored the majority opinion holding that requiring
  public-sector employees to pay agency fees to a union violates the First Amendment,
  overruling *Abood v. Detroit Board of Education*.
- **McDonald v. City of Chicago (2010)** — wrote the plurality opinion holding that the
  Second Amendment's individual right to keep and bear arms applies to the states
  through the Fourteenth Amendment.
- **Snyder v. Phelps (2011)** — filed a lone dissent arguing that the First Amendment
  should not shield the Westboro Baptist Church from tort liability for protests staged
  near a fallen Marine's funeral.
