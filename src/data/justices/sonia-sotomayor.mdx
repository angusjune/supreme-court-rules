## Background

Sonia Maria Sotomayor was born on June 25, 1954, in the Bronx, New York City, to parents who had moved to New York from Puerto Rico. Her father died when she was nine, and she was raised largely by her mother, a nurse. Diagnosed with type 1 diabetes in childhood, she has spoken often about how that experience shaped her discipline and her interest in the law.

She attended Princeton University, graduating summa cum laude in 1976, where she was elected to Phi Beta Kappa and won the Pyne Prize, the university's top undergraduate award. She earned her J.D. from Yale Law School in 1979, where she served as an editor of the Yale Law Journal.

Sotomayor began her career as an Assistant District Attorney in Manhattan under District Attorney Robert Morgenthau, prosecuting cases from 1979 to 1984. She then entered private practice at the firm Pavia & Harcourt, working on commercial litigation and intellectual property matters and eventually becoming a partner.

Her path to the bench spanned three presidents. In 1992, President George H.W. Bush appointed her to the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York, making her the first Hispanic federal judge in New York State; as a trial judge she issued the 1995 injunction widely credited with ending the Major League Baseball strike. In 1998, President Bill Clinton elevated her to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. President Barack Obama nominated her to the Supreme Court on May 26, 2009, to fill the seat of the retiring Justice David Souter. The Senate confirmed her by a vote of 68-31 on August 6, 2009, making her the first Hispanic and Latina justice and the third woman to serve on the Court.

## Jurisprudence

Sotomayor sits firmly within the Court's liberal wing. Following the death of Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg in 2020 and the retirement of Justice Stephen Breyer in 2022, she became the senior-most member of the Court's three-justice liberal bloc, alongside Justices Elena Kagan and Ketanji Brown Jackson.

Her approach is generally pragmatic and fact-intensive, with sustained attention to criminal justice, the Fourth Amendment, racial equality, immigration, and the practical consequences that legal rules carry for ordinary people. In the conservative-majority era she has become one of the Court's most prominent and frequently quoted dissenters, often writing separately to register sharp disagreement and to spell out the real-world stakes of the majority's reasoning.

## Notable rulings

- **J.D.B. v. North Carolina (2011)** — Wrote the majority opinion holding that a child's age is a relevant factor in determining whether a suspect is "in custody" for Miranda purposes, extending those protections to account for the vulnerability of juveniles.
- **Schuette v. BAMN (2014)** — Authored a lengthy dissent (joined by Justice Ginsburg) defending race-conscious admissions and arguing that courts should confront questions of race directly, a statement widely regarded as a signature expression of her views.
- **Utah v. Strieff (2016)** — Dissented from the Court's narrowing of the exclusionary rule, including a widely cited passage on the human cost of unlawful police stops, anchoring one of her most-quoted criminal-justice opinions.
- **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022)** — Joined the joint dissent with Justices Breyer and Kagan protesting the Court's decision to overrule Roe v. Wade and eliminate the constitutional right to abortion.
- **Trump v. United States (2024)** — Authored the principal dissent rejecting broad criminal immunity for former presidents, closing with the line "With fear for our democracy, I dissent."
